Connection 01 / 06
Power Is the New Location
AI Data Center RE Boom → Grid as Site Selector
What's happening
Access to reliable, affordable power has overtaken connectivity as the #1 factor in data center site selection. Bloom Energy's 2025 survey confirmed this shift. With 48 GW of US data center capacity under construction, landlords and developers in power-rich regions are gaining a structural advantage over gateway markets.
43% corporate power procurement
100 GW planned US capacity
PJM wholesale prices +45% YoY
Why it's unexpected
Traditionally, data centers chased fiber hubs and tax incentives. Now they're chasing substations. This is reshaping industrial real estate geography in ways that bypass traditional gateway markets entirely — rural areas near power generation are suddenly prime RE targets.
⊳ Strategic Planning Imperative
Map your commercial portfolio and development pipeline against grid capacity, not just demographics. Partner with utilities early. Regions able to offer fast, clean grid connections in 2026 will command a 3–5 year first-mover window in data center leasing before bottlenecks ease.

Connection 02 / 06
The Climate–Insurance–Migration Doom Loop
Sun Belt Price Collapse → Climate Volatility Spiral
What's happening
Florida and Colorado escrow costs jumped 55–57% in 2025, driven by climate-linked insurance premiums. Cotality data shows that where property taxes and insurance rise fastest, mortgage delinquencies spike. This is triggering out-migration from Sun Belt markets — the same markets that were the biggest real estate boom stories of 2021–2023.
+55% FL insurance escrow
+8% national premium forecast
In-migration to FL/TX stalled
Why it's unexpected
The energy-to-insurance connection is indirect but powerful: rising extreme weather events (driven by energy consumption patterns globally) are making certain property markets literally uninsurable — not in 20 years, but now. The feedback loop accelerates as insurers withdraw and property values crater.
⊳ Strategic Planning Imperative
Stress-test your Sun Belt portfolio for insurance cost trajectory, not just cap rates. Physical climate risk underwriting should be built into acquisition models. The Midwest and Northeast — historically less exciting — may offer the most durable returns as climate-resilience becomes a pricing premium.
Risk Level
High
Time Horizon
3–5 yr
Connection 03 / 06
Build-to-Rent Becomes an Energy Asset
Institutional BTR Surge → Distributed Energy Buildout
What's happening
Institutional investors now account for roughly one-third of all single-family home purchases. Unlike individual homeowners, these players are designing communities at scale — and they're integrating solar + battery storage from the ground up, creating private micro-grids that reduce operating costs and command rental premiums.
⅓ of SF purchases = institutional
54 GW new US capacity (2025)
Solar lead time: 14–24 months
Why it's unexpected
Residential real estate has never historically been an energy infrastructure play. The shift is creating a new asset class: BTR communities that generate, store, and even sell energy back to the grid. As electricity prices rise 32% over the past decade, energy cost control becomes a genuine competitive moat in rental housing.
⊳ Strategic Planning Imperative
If you're developing or operating rental communities, model energy infrastructure as a capital expenditure that generates direct return — not just a sustainability checkbox. Partnerships with community solar and battery developers could meaningfully compress operating costs while differentiating the product.

Connection 04 / 06
The Copper Deficit Is a Construction Crisis
Stalled Affordability → Copper Supply Deficit
What's happening
The copper market swings into a 1-million-metric-ton supply deficit in 2026. Copper is essential for EV charging infrastructure, building electrification, grid wiring, and new construction. As data center demand and EV rollout collide with slow mine permitting, construction cost inflation will compound the housing affordability crisis that is already 35% below pre-COVID index levels.
−1M MT deficit in 2026
35% below affordability index
6%+ mortgage rates persist
Why it's unexpected
The housing affordability story is usually told through mortgage rates and home prices. But a commodity-level supply crunch in copper — driven by the energy transition and AI buildout — will add a structural cost floor to new construction that rate cuts alone cannot overcome.
⊳ Strategic Planning Imperative
Lock in material procurement contracts early for 2026–2027 development pipelines. Explore modular construction methods that reduce copper intensity. For affordable housing developers, the copper squeeze is a genuine existential variable — escalation clauses and supply chain hedging should be standard in all new contracts.
Risk Level
High
Connection 05 / 06
LNG Boom Creates a Gulf Coast RE Surge
CRE Revival → LNG Export Expansion
What's happening
The Golden Pass LNG project in Texas and the broader LNG export push are creating industrial real estate demand along the Gulf Coast that is largely invisible to residential market watchers. Pro-development policy and reopened LNG permitting under the current administration are catalyzing worker housing, logistics infrastructure, and industrial park development in previously overlooked coastal Texas and Louisiana markets.
Golden Pass: 5.2M tons/yr
8M tons new LNG capacity 2026
Energy M&A up 27% in 2025
Why it's unexpected
Commodity infrastructure plays typically don't move residential and mixed-use real estate — but mega-projects on this scale generate 10,000+ worker-years and permanently alter the demographics of surrounding communities. Houston already ranks in the top 5 on PwC's Emerging Trends report for 2026.
⊳ Strategic Planning Imperative
Identify workforce housing and light industrial opportunities within 25–50 miles of major LNG and pipeline construction zones along the Gulf Coast. These markets are underfollowed by institutional capital and offer early-entry pricing with a multi-year demand tailwind from infrastructure buildout.

Connection 06 / 06
The Rate Trap Is an Energy Demand Dispersal
Stalled Affordability → Grid Stress in Commuter Markets
What's happening
With mortgage rates stuck above 6%, buyers are locked out of urban cores and moving to lower-cost exurban and rural markets. These areas often have older, less resilient grid infrastructure. As EV adoption grows simultaneously, electricity demand is spiking in markets least prepared to handle it — creating both energy reliability risk and investment opportunity.
Mortgage apps up 31% YoY
Electricity prices +32% (10 yr)
377 GW interconnection backlog
Why it's unexpected
High mortgage rates are conventionally a real estate story. But they're quietly reshaping energy infrastructure demand in secondary markets. Builders offering rate buydowns (now standard at 4–5%) are concentrating new supply in exurban areas that are simultaneously experiencing EV grid strain and renewable energy development — creating complex, overlapping pressures on local utilities.
⊳ Strategic Planning Imperative
For developers building in secondary and tertiary markets: treat grid capacity as a due-diligence item equivalent to zoning. Partner with local utilities during entitlement, not after. Communities that can offer EV-ready infrastructure with distributed energy will command a premium as the rate trap sends buyers further from city centers through 2027.
